Yona of the Dawn by Mizuho Kusanagi


"Within my tiny world, I protected my tiny happiness." - Yona


"Yona reels from the shock of witnessing a loved one’s murder and having to fight for her life. With Hak’s help, she flees the palace and struggles to survive while evading her enemy’s forces. But where will this displaced princess go when all the paths before her are uncertain?" - official synopsis

This review may contain spoilers up to volume 39. Please proceed carefully.

This is my shojo One Piece. It was a painstaking journey to get my hands on all the volumes so I could binge read it. Oh, but that journey was well worth it. I couldn’t read it fast enough and I hated how quickly I read it. From the gripping plot to the lovable characters, this whole series had me sat and my eyes glued to the pages.

What makes this series so breathtakingly wonderful, is watching Yona go from a spoiled sheltered princess, to someone worthy of being called the Crimson Dragon King. After her father is murdered by her cousin (who is also unfortunately her crush) she and her personal guard, Hak, flee the palace. Faced with the reality of her cousin’s betrayal, Yona has no choice but to accept her new life, meaningless as it has now become.

But not for long. As she soon finds purpose from a lone priest who tells her she can reclaim her kingdom once she reunites the Four Dragon Warriors of Kouka. Which is like, okay a pretty standard fantasy quest. And yes, it is, but this journey is not without its fault. It’s also laced with a healthy dose of comedic relief and the cutest squirrel companion.

During their days of travel to gather the Dragon Warriors, Yona asks Hak to teach her how to use a sword. Which he refuses, because fighting his job, not a princess’s. Though after some pestering, he agrees to teach her how to use a bow. It’s not long that she picks it up, able to hunt down a bird in flight or an immoral lord trafficking young women in a port town.

Rounding up the Dragon Warriors is but one task. So what comes after? Yona’s only home had once been the palace, but now she’s grown accustomed to life always on the move, danger lurking around every corner. But with that came the knowledge of Kouka’s true state—the one she’d been ignorant to her whole life. She finds it wrong to reclaim the kingdom while her people suffer. So for now, she’ll aid them in whatever way possible—even it means occasionally helping the one who betrayed her.

Seeing this gradual growth of a young girl, grappling with love all the while, is in a way endearing. Yona’s story will always hold a special place in my heart.
