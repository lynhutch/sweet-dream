The Love Hypothesis by Ali Hazelwood


"You can fall in love: someone will catch you." - Ali Hazelwood


"As a third-year Ph.D. candidate, Olive Smith doesn’t believe in lasting romantic relationships–but her best friend does, and that’s what got her into this situation. Convincing Anh that Olive is dating and well on her way to a happily ever after was always going to take more than hand-wavy Jedi mind tricks: Scientists require proof. So, like any self-respecting biologist, Olive panics and kisses the first man she sees.

That man is none other than Adam Carlsen, a young hotshot professor–and well-known ass. Which is why Olive is positively floored when Stanford’s reigning lab tyrant agrees to keep her charade a secret and be her fake boyfriend. But when a big science conference goes haywire, putting Olive’s career on the Bunsen burner, Adam surprises her again with his unyielding support and even more unyielding…six-pack abs.

Suddenly their little experiment feels dangerously close to combustion. And Olive discovers that the only thing more complicated than a hypothesis on love is putting her own heart under the microscope." - official synopsis

This review contains minor spoilers. Please proceed carefully.

This was written for STEM girlies by a STEM girly. Even though I’m not a science person this still had me kicking my feet. The actual biology talk didn’t even go too in depth so I didn’t feel absolutely clueless reading this. It’s not a ground-breaking story, but it’s a niche romance we need more of.

This book stands as evidence to support MY hypothesis: fanfic writers who pursue publication make great books. Sure some parts of this book made it obvious that this story was once a fanfic, but did I care? No, absolutely not. During a period of sparse reading, I devoured this book. I giggled and kicked my feet and rolled around on my bed during each and every cute little interaction between Olive and Adam.

One thing about me is that I love a good fake-dating trope. Depending on its execution, its the perfect recipe for tension so thick you’d have to be jellyfish to not notice it. All of these little moments building up until they finally boil over in one very long, very detailed chapter.

Now this book might focus on biology, but these character’s have undeniable chemistry. It’s so good that I’m willing to ignore the miscommunication trope sprinkled in.

Adam is notorious for his cold and blunt attitude towards his students, but its a common case of “sweet to the one he actually cares about.” Which I am always weak to when it comes to a romance.

But navigating a fake relationship isn’t the only thing this book has to offer.

One thing this book handled really well was the fear every woman harbors: predatory men in the workplace. Being led to believe the opportunity you’ve been waiting for is finally within reach, but the one dangling it over you head as attached an ulterior motive. And you can’t have one without giving the other. In a perfect world, this would never happen, but even this story can’t exist in a perfect world. The ending, however, is exactly what should happen in every case.

Despite the stories origins, which is something many in the publishing industry are trying to repaint, it’s truly a wonderfully written romance. And one I want to revisit soon.
