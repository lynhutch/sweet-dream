The Foxhole Court (All For the Game) By Nora Sakavic


"Sunrise, Abrahm, death; these are truths." - Andrew Minyard


"Neil Josten is the newest addition to the Palmetto State University Exy team. He's short, he's fast, he's got a ton of potential—and he's the runaway son of the murderous crime lord known as The Butcher.

Signing a contract with the PSU Foxes is the last thing a guy like Neil should do. The team is high profile and he doesn't need sports crews broadcasting pictures of his face around the nation. His lies will hold up only so long under this kind of scrutiny and the truth will get him killed.

But Neil's not the only one with secrets on the team. One of Neil's new teammates is a friend from his old life, and Neil can't walk away from him a second time. Neil has survived the last eight years by running. Maybe he's finally found someone and something worth fighting for." - official synopsis

This review contains spoilers for the entirety of the All For the Game series. Please proceed carefully.

Before reading the books, consult the extensive list of trigger warnings.

This is not a good book. But it is my favorite. And, to me, that made it worth the read. Okay, you know, to say that it isn’t a good book really isn’t fair. It’s definitely not something I would recommend to just anyone, but it holds an incredibly special place in my heart. And for that, this series is precious.

Right off the bat, I’m gonna say it: this series reads like a sports anime. As if a cross between Haikyuu and Banana Fish but with a newly made and wildly popular fictitious sport. I mean it’s got everything. Drama, romance, college au, team building, and mafias. What more could you want in a book?

Niel Josten is the epitome of unreliable narrators and poor decision-makers. Low profile does not mean going to a high-profile school to play a silly little sport with your childhood friend who is tied to the yakuza family that is hunting you down. However, I will hand it to him since Kevin Day was somehow fooled by his disguise of box hair dye and cheap colored contacts. Granted, Kevin was going through his own personal hell.

In the end, Neil does decide to join the exy team of a high-profile university, Palmetto State University, effectively ignoring his mother’s dying words. High-profile only because that team is dwindling in performance and constantly mocked by the college athletics association. Not to mention their historic rivals, the Edgar Allan Ravens, have joined their district and a fated match looms overhead. But that’s not all. The son of the Yakuza head who’s tied to his father, a mafia boss named “the Butcher”, plays for that rival team.

The Ravens are the national undefeated champions, but that doesn’t mean the Foxes are willing to take a beating lying down. With Kevin, a former Raven, as their new assistant coach, they now have a special mousekatool at their disposal. Now this all seems like a very good set-up, right? Well, it gets worse. This team has absolutely zero chemistry and their own personal problems. Many of which they have yet to work through.

But that isn’t where it all starts going downhill. No, it begins when Neil decides to provoke Riko, son of the Yakuza head, on live national television. But his disguise is so good. Surely this won’t bring about anything bad later. No, of course not. Why would it? Neil is the poster child of smart, well-thought out decisions.

Despite how much he tries, Neil lets slip little bits of his hidden past. The kicker is at a banquet, where the Foxes and Ravens are seated near each other and Jean, Kevin, and Neil end up in private conversation in French. Becuase Neil just casually knows French. On top of German. And that isn’t the least bit suspicious with his walled-up nature.

All of this said, as the story progresses, and following the death of one of the Foxes, they end growing closer as a team. Steadily becoming one solid unit. Neil and Andrew quietly growing closer to each other than the rest. Until Neil is backed into a corner and is faced with no other options than to leave the foxes. This is but one of two climaxes in the final book. Which leads me to my next point.

The last book is so beautifully well-done that I still, to this day, can’t stopping thinking about how it was structured. The first climax deals with Neil and his father. It’s bloody and gruesome, but he makes it out. At this point, Neil can no longer hide his true identity from the other Foxes and is forced to bear all when reunited. After a brief reprieve, allowing Neil time to heal and momentarily relax, it’s time for the final climax: Foxes vs Ravens. Neil’s past was one story arc, but exy has always been important to Neil, so it’s no surprise we have to see the final showdown to the end.

Without a doubt, this is a majorly character-driven story. The plot serves to develop the characters, mainly Neil. For that reason, it won’t appeal to everyone. Even I don’t tend to gravitate towards these types of stories. But this one was so done I couldn’t put it down and ached to reread it as soon as I finished it. All-in-all it was well worth my time, and with the announcement of another book, I’m already planning my next reread.
