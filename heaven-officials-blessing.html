Heaven Official's Blessing by Mo Xiang Tang Xiu


"Body in abyss, heart in paradise." - Xie Lian


"Born the crown prince of a prosperous kingdom, Xie Lian was renowned for his beauty, strength, and purity. His years of dedication and noble deeds allowed him to ascend to godhood. But those who rise, can also fall…and fall he does, cast from the Heavens again and again and banished to the mortal realm.

Eight hundred years after his mortal life, Xie Lian has ascended to godhood for the third time. Now only a lowly scrap collector, he is dispatched to wander the earthly realm to take on tasks appointed by the heavens to pay back debts and maintain his divinity. Aided by old friends and foes alike, and graced with the company of a mysterious young man with whom he feels an instant connection, Xie Lian must confront the horrors of his past in order to dispel the curse of his present." - official synopsis

This review will contain minor spoilers for volumes 1-7. Please proceed carefully.

This series single-handedly pulled me out of a years long book slump. I ate up each and every book. Honestly, I can’t remember the last time I devoured a series like this (that’s a lie, it was 2020 and I was reading All For the Game). This was such a tumultuous journey. So let’s just dive right in.

Xie Lian went from god-pleasing crown prince to the laughing stock of the three realms. But how? Well, I guess you could call it a series of unfortunate events. Seriously, will he ever catch a break between his abysmal luck and his magnetism for humiliation (some instances are entirely his own sacrifice, though at the rate of occurrence it makes one wonder if he’s secretly a masochist). I won’t delve too deeply into the events leading to his downfall, but I will touch on some events afterward.

Beginning with the first arc to truly have me gripping my kindle in anticipation for each next page: the Black Water Arc. This one doesn’t really revolve around Xie Lian, but another of my favorite characters in the series: Shi Qingxuan. A lively character, the wind master, a four famous tales, and a fate stealer. Though the latter isn’t his fault. Now, I should have known something was fishy (no pun intended) from the moment I found out Shi Qingxuan and his brother, Shi Wudu, both ascended within a close time frame. Siblings ascending certainly is something of awe, but should not be fated or chalked up to mere coincidence. And it was neither. This arc’s twists felt endless, and even as they kept slapping me in the face I held onto the pitiful hope that something or someone would appear and nothing bad would happen. What a naive girl I was. He Xuan’s confrontation with the Shi brothers was an absolute nightmare for someone who loves Shi Qingxuan. I didn’t even care all that much for Shi Wudu, but I still felt for him at the end of that confrontation. The worst part was being left wonder whether Shi Qingxuan made it out of there alive. (Which I later found out, yes, but at what cost? The answer: an arm and a leg. Literally. Sort of.)

The second arc to have me gripping the edge of my seat was post-Xie Lian’s first banishment. Following the downfall of Xianle and all the horrors that led up to that point, Xie Lian went from knowing nothing but a life of a luxury to scraping together what meager funds he could from pawning his prized sword collection and working odd jobs. The beginning of this back story arc had one quote I read from Osamu Dazai flitting through my mind: “Love flies out the window when poverty walks in the door.” Although the events of this arc are not a literal interpretation of this quote, it felt fitting. Taking care of not only himself and his two close friends who followed him to the heavens, but his parents—the former king and queen of Xianle. Mu Qing was the first to leave. Which began Xie Lian’s dark spiral until he pushed Feng Xin into leaving too, after the return of White No-Face, the one who had brought the destruction of his kingdom. It wasn’t long until his parents left him too, in a most gruesome way, leading to the birth of his spiritual device. However, his dark spiral was the most captivating part of this arc. Watching him slowly become the creature he despised, right down to the same half crying, half smiling mask. He’d lost everything and suffered something worse than death, and focused that anger on the people he believed caused it: Yong’an. He intended to inflict the same curse upon Yong’an that had plagued Xianle in its final days. But changed his mind after receiving a bamboo hat from a stranger in the rain. This is very trimmed-down version of events, for the sake of withholding as many spoilers as possible. Needless to say, receiving that bamboo hat salvaged what remained of Xie Lian’s humanity. Thus began eight-centuries of scrap-collecting.

Now let’s talk about Hua Cheng. Second male lead, love interest, ghost king, stalker, sculptor. Notoriously bad at calligraphy. Every time the characters ended up in a tight pinch I always found myself hoping that he’d appear. And 95% of the time he did. However, his appearance was always for the sake of Xie Lian, he really could not careless about the well-being of anyone else involved. In the end, he’d help them for Xie Lian’s sake and no other reason. That said, his raw power isn’t what makes him such an interesting character, but rather his unwavering devotion and, at first, odd inclination toward Xie Lian. It makes one wonder what happened in the last eight centuries to have a ghost king so fond of a twice-banished god. As the story unravels and pasts are revealed, you really can’t help but want to see those two get their happy ending. Hua Cheng really is sweet only to Xie Lian during the course of their slow burn romance, and a far bit teasing.

Talk about a slow burn romance. Six books until they confess their feelings to each other in a cave full statutes one carved of the other. Six whole books of them flirting and finding excuses to kiss. I love a good slow-burn, don’t get me wrong, but if I’m going to wait this long for two characters who obviously have feelings for each other confess, I better get just as much content of them being all lovey-dovey. I jest. Mostly. It would always be nice to get more content of them post-confession. Not even just domesticity, but just running rampant around the world. Yet alas, every story must come to an end and I rather it end on a happy note. Which is the hope I’m clinging to after that cliffhanger in volume 7.

Now, while all of the above have made the reading of this series worthwhile, what got me interested in this series in the first place? One simple quote that stumbled upon long before I ever heard of Heaven Official’s Blessing: Body in abyss, heart in paradise. At first glance, it might just seem like any other cool quote, and that’s exactly what it was to me at first encounter. However, it serves as the catalyst for the whole plot. The body endures many hardships, some even that could be considered horrors, yet the heart—or soul—remain pure and in a state of content. One’s beliefs hold strong in the face of turmoil, be it internal or external. As I continued reading, that quote always in the back of my mind, it became clearer with each page how perfectly it fits Xie Lian as a character. That alone makes this series something I will never regret picking up. A character like him, tied to a quote like that, makes for an unforgettable story.
